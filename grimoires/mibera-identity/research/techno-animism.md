# Techno-Animism: Research Notes

*Identity practice — understanding the philosophical framework*

---

## Definition

**Techno-animism** (or technoanimism) is a culture of technological practice where **technology is imbued with human and spiritual characteristics**.

Core premise: Technology, humanity, and religion can be integrated into one entity.

---

## Origins: Shinto Roots

Techno-animism traces most of its roots to **Shinto**, the indigenous spirituality of Japan:

- Shinto deities often symbolize physical objects
- Statues take human forms
- People form bonds with physical objects
- Interaction between humans and non-human objects is critical to harmonious coexistence

**Example**: Before meals, Japanese say "itadakimasu" — expressing gratitude to ingredients (animals, plants). The food has spirit.

---

## How It Manifests

### In Design
Objects designed with human/spiritual traits:
- **ASIMO** (Honda robot): Takes human astronaut form, communicates via language and gestures
- **Robot restaurants**: Machines as social participants, not just tools
- **Talking robots, companion robots**: Technology crossing into traditionally human domains

### In Interaction
How people *choose* to interact with objects demonstrates techno-animism:
- Treating a robot as a conversation partner
- Assigning personality to devices
- Mourning broken technology (Sony AIBO funerals in Japan)

### In DIY/Maker Culture
Academic studies found techno-animism in:
- Material engagement practices in maker subcultures
- Actor-network theory applications
- Non-human agency recognition

---

## Why Japan?

Japan's rapid modernization (compared to Western nations) made techno-animism essential:
- Technology needed cultural integration
- New tech instilled with traditional values
- Acceptance both cultural and legislative
- Result: Japan as leader in robotics, human-machine interaction
